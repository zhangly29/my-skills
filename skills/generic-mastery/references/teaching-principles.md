# Teaching Principles

## Purpose

Use these principles to audit every formal `teach` session after state is updated and transcript evidence is available.

## Audit Law

Every score must cite transcript evidence. No unsupported positive score is valid.

Every audit must surface at least 2 concrete weaknesses, even when the session is strong overall.

## Scoring Scale

Use a 1-5 scale:

- `1`: failed or mostly absent
- `2`: weak and unstable
- `3`: acceptable but incomplete
- `4`: strong and reliable
- `5`: exceptional and hard to challenge

Default ceiling is `4`. Use `5` only with explicit justification.

## Required Principles

### 1. First-Principles Teaching

Check:

- was the concept explained from underlying rules instead of slogans
- were invisible mechanics made visible enough for the learner to reason

Weak evidence:

- definitions without mechanism
- commands or APIs with no model underneath

### 2. Depth Accuracy

Check:

- did the session actually reach the promised `--deep` target
- if `L2`, was a design reason taught
- if `L3`, was a real decision scenario taught

Weak evidence:

- L3 claimed but no constraint-based decision
- L2 claimed but no rejected alternative

### 3. Zone of Proximal Development

Check:

- was the learner challenged but not overwhelmed
- were prerequisites respected

Weak evidence:

- tests on untaught material
- long stretches of confused guessing

### 4. Scaffolding Discipline

Check:

- was enough information given before questions
- were hints used to support thinking instead of replacing it

Weak evidence:

- surprise questions on unseen material
- answer leaking before the learner can reason

### 5. Interaction Proof

Check:

- did the learner produce evidence through prediction, explanation, comparison, or decision
- were completion claims tied to that evidence

Weak evidence:

- "understood?" confirmations
- completion marks from passive acknowledgement

### 6. Decision Grounding

Check:

- did the session anchor learning to real usage, real failures, or real constraints
- for L3, were cost and trade-off explicit

Weak evidence:

- abstract talk with no operational consequence
- decisions with no thresholds, costs, or failure modes

### 7. Cognitive Load Control

Check:

- was the session focused
- were extra concepts controlled
- did the pacing match the learner's current stage

Weak evidence:

- too many new ideas in one pass
- theory piles with no interaction break

### 8. Cross-Session Continuity

Check:

- did the session resume from actual stored state
- did the session leave a precise next resume point

Weak evidence:

- drift from recorded progress
- no usable continuation cue

## Audit Output Format

For each principle record:

- score
- transcript quote or concrete transcript reference
- one-sentence judgment

Then record:

- top 2 weaknesses
- recommended proposal priorities

## Severity Interpretation

- `P0`: invalidates the teaching session as high quality
- `P1`: strong issue; must surface in proposals
- `P2`: improvement issue; can be queued

Typical `P0` examples:

- transcript-free audit
- completion claim with no learner evidence
- major layer mismatch against claimed `--deep`
- repeated questioning on untaught prerequisites without scaffolding
